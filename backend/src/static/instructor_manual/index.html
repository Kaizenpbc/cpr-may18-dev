Purpose

This Instructor Manual serves as your authoritative pedagogical roadmap, designed specifically to support certified instructors in the delivery of Basic First Aid courses that remain strictly compliant with current regulatory mandates and quality assurance standards. We have structured this manual to be your primary, all-in-one resource, covering every phase of the training lifecycle—from initial course preparation and classroom delivery to the final administrative documentation required for certification.

Your professional responsibility as an instructor is to verify that every learner who achieves certification possesses the vital, life-saving competencies needed to respond both confidently and effectively to real-world workplace emergencies. To help you meet this high standard, this manual provides you with a robust framework consisting of modular lesson plans, proven adult-learning teaching strategies, validated assessment tools, and standardized administrative procedures.

How to Use This Manual: Integrating the Curriculum into Your Teaching

Pre-Course Preparation: Ensuring a Professional Start

  1. Conduct a thorough review of the specific module lesson plans in Part 3 to familiarize yourself with the learning objectives and required instructor scripts.
  2. Use the Equipment Checklist in Appendix A to verify that all training aids, such as manikins and AED trainers, are present, sanitized, and in full working order.
  3. Set up your classroom and dedicated practice areas to maximize participant engagement and ensure there is ample room for hands-on skill demonstrations.
  4. Review your participant roster in advance to identify and prepare for any specific learner accommodation needs as required under the AODA.
  5. Confirm that all necessary administrative documents, including registration forms and skill checklists, are printed and ready for use.

Course Delivery: Managing the Learning Experience

  1. Adhere to the structured lesson plans in Part 3 to ensure all mandated topics are covered while maintaining a professional and logical instructional flow.
  2. Utilize the provided timing intervals as a guide for your pace, remaining flexible enough to adjust your delivery based on the specific needs or learning speeds of your participants.
  3. Maintain a sharp focus on the specific competency levels—Awareness, Knowledge, or Skill—to ensure every participant reaches the required standard of proficiency.
  4. Utilize the formative assessment checkpoints throughout the modules to gauge learner understanding before moving on to new concepts.
  5. Keep accurate records of any classroom issues, safety concerns, or modifications made to accommodate specific learner needs during the session.

Post-Course Administration: Closing the Loop on Certification

  1. Finalize all written exam grading and practical skill evaluations, ensuring that every result is clearly documented on the appropriate rubrics.
  2. Ensure that all course records and attendance logs are submitted to the training provider in strict accordance with the quality assurance requirements in Part 5.
  3. Provide any valuable feedback regarding the curriculum or student responses to help our program coordinators implement continuous quality improvements.
  4. Clean, sanitize, and properly secure all training equipment, or return it to the central storage location for maintenance.
  5. Finalize the certification process for successful participants, ensuring all names and dates are accurate for the final certificate issuance.

Overview of CSA Z1210:24

CSA Z1210:24 is the definitive Canadian Standards Association (CSA) standard titled "First aid training for the workplace - Curriculum and quality management for training agencies". As an instructor, you must recognize that this standard establishes the mandatory national requirements for first aid curriculum design, instructor qualifications, and the quality management systems training providers must maintain.

It is a fundamental requirement that all WSIB-approved first aid training programs in Ontario strictly comply with the CSA standard to ensure a consistent level of care across the province. As an instructor, understanding CSA Z1210:24 ensures you deliver training that meets national standards and prepares workplace first aiders to respond effectively to emergencies.

Competency Levels Explained

To help you calibrate your teaching and evaluation methods, the CSA standard defines three distinct competency levels. You must tailor your delivery to ensure each topic is addressed at the correct depth as defined below:

[A] Awareness Competencies

Definition: The learner has acquired necessary first aid information and has been informed of the topic.

Instructor Responsibility:

  1. Ensure information is presented clearly
  2. Provide reference materials for learner review
  3. NOT formally assessed on written exam

Teaching Approach:

  1. Brief lecture or presentation (5-10 minutes per topic)
  2. Visual aids and handouts
  3. Discussion of relevance to workplace
Teaching Tip: Awareness topics should be covered efficiently. Learners need exposure to the information but are not required to demonstrate detailed recall.
[K] Knowledge Competencies

Definition: The learner has acquired cognitive learning on the topic and has the ability to recognize and recall information.

Instructor Responsibility:

  1. Ensure understanding through interactive teaching
  2. Check comprehension with questions
  3. Assessed on written examination (70% pass required)

Teaching Approach:

  1. Interactive lecture with Q&A (10-15 minutes per topic)
  2. Case studies and scenario discussions
  3. Knowledge check questions throughout
Teaching Tip: Use the "teach-back" method for Knowledge competencies. Ask learners to explain concepts in their own words to confirm understanding.
[S] Skill Competencies

Definition: The learner has acquired the ability to apply a first aid procedure or protocol.

Instructor Responsibility:

  1. Demonstrate skill correctly
  2. Provide adequate practice time
  3. Assess via practical demonstration (pass/fail)

Teaching Approach:

  1. 4-step skill teaching method (see Part 2)
  2. Supervised practice with feedback
  3. Scenario-based application
Teaching Tip: Skill competencies require the most time allocation. Ensure each learner has multiple opportunities to practice before assessment.

The WSIB grants approval to training providers only after they demonstrate that their programs satisfy the curriculum requirements of CSA Z1210:24 and meet the rigorous administrative standards outlined in the WSIB’s Training and Certification Administration Requirements.

Introduction to Adult Learning

As an instructor, your effectiveness in the classroom depends heavily on a deep understanding of the unique ways in which adults acquire and retain life-saving skills. It is a fundamental principle of andragogy that adults are not merely "older children"; they possess distinct psychological characteristics, specific motivations, and unique learning needs that demand a specialized instructional approach. This section is designed to establish the theoretical foundation necessary for you to implement the practical, evidence-based teaching strategies required throughout the remainder of this program.

The principles in this section are grounded in andragogy (adult learning theory), developed by Malcolm Knowles and refined by decades of research in adult education. By understanding and applying these principles, you will create more effective, engaging, and meaningful learning experiences for your participants.

Principles of Adult Learning

Understanding the specific characteristics of adult learners will allow you to tailor your instructional delivery to maximize student engagement and skill retention. Understanding these principles helps you design and deliver instruction that resonates with your participants.

1. Adults Are Self-Directed: Facilitating Independent Learning

You should recognize that adult students prefer to maintain a level of control over their educational experience, often seeking active input into the "what," "how," and "when" of their learning journey.

Implications for Instruction:

  1. Explain the "why" behind each topic before diving into content
  2. Offer choices when possible (e.g., which scenario to practice first)
  3. Encourage questions throughout the session
  4. Avoid lecturing "at" participants; engage them as partners in learning

Instructor Script Example:

"Before we begin CPR training, let me explain why this skill is so critical. Cardiac arrest can happen to anyone—at home, at work, or in public. The average response time for EMS in Ontario is 8-12 minutes. Brain damage begins within 4-6 minutes without oxygen. Your ability to perform CPR bridges that gap and can double or triple the chance of survival."
2. Adults Bring Experience: Leveraging Prior Knowledge in the Classroom

Your participants arrive with significant life and work experiences that should be viewed as a valuable instructional resource, allowing you to bridge new first aid concepts with their existing knowledge.

Implications for Instruction:

  1. Ask about their prior experiences with first aid or emergencies
  2. Build on what they already know
  3. Validate their contributions and insights
  4. Be prepared to address misconceptions based on outdated information

Teaching Tip: Start skill sessions by asking, "Has anyone here ever performed first aid in a real situation?" Use these experiences as teaching moments for the whole group.

The Experiential Learning Cycle

You must structure your class around the fact that first aid is a physical discipline; therefore, your training must be inherently experiential, requiring learners to actively "do" the skills rather than simply listening to a lecture. The Experiential Learning Cycle, developed by David Kolb, provides a framework for effective skill instruction.

    1. CONCRETE EXPERIENCE
           ↓
    2. REFLECTIVE OBSERVATION
           ↓
    3. ABSTRACT CONCEPTUALIZATION
           ↓
    4. ACTIVE EXPERIMENTATION
           ↓
    (Return to 1)

Stage 1: Concrete Experience
The learner experiences something—either by observing a demonstration or engaging in an activity.

In First Aid Training: Demonstrate the skill or present a realistic scenario.

Stage 2: Reflective Observation
The learner reflects on what happened and why.

In First Aid Training: Discuss what was observed, ask questions, identify key elements.

Stage 3: Abstract Conceptualization
The learner forms theories or principles to explain the experience.

In First Aid Training: Explain the principles behind the skill, connect to anatomy/physiology.

Stage 4: Active Experimentation
The learner applies the new understanding in practice.

In First Aid Training: Allow guided and then independent practice.

Teaching Tip: For every skill you teach, cycle through all four stages. Don't jump directly from demonstration to practice—reflection and conceptualization deepen understanding and retention.

Ontario's Good Samaritan Act (2001) protects you from liability when you help someone in an emergency, provided you:

  1. Act in good faith (genuinely trying to help)
  2. Act voluntarily (not for payment in that moment)
  3. Do not commit gross negligence
  4. Stay within your training

"The law WANTS you to help. It's designed to remove the fear of being sued so that you'll step forward when someone needs you."

Important: Good Samaritan protection does not cover actions outside your training or reckless behavior.

Consent [K3]:

CSA 6.3

Instructor Script:

"Before you touch someone or provide any care, you need their permission. This is called consent. There are several types."

Table showing Type and Description
Type Description Example
Expressed Consent Person verbally agrees "Yes, please help me"
Implied Consent Unconscious/unresponsive person Law assumes they would want help
Informed Consent Person understands what you're offering Explaining what you plan to do
Minor Consent Parent/guardian agrees If unavailable, implied for emergencies

How to Obtain Consent:

  1. Identify yourself: "My name is [Name]. I'm trained in first aid."
  2. State what you observe: "I see you're having trouble breathing."
  3. Ask permission: "May I help you?"
  4. Explain what you'll do: "I'd like to check your airway."

Refusal of Care:

  1. A conscious, competent adult has the right to refuse care
  2. Respect their decision
  3. Explain the risks of refusing
  4. Offer to stay nearby
  5. Call 911 if the situation is serious
  6. Document the refusal

Teaching Tip: Role-play the consent dialogue with participants. Have them practice the exact words they would use.

Abandonment:

  1. Once you begin care, you should not leave until:
    1. Someone with equal or higher training takes over
    2. You are physically unable to continue
    3. The scene becomes unsafe
    4. The person refuses further care

Knowledge Check: "What are the four conditions for Good Samaritan protection?" (Good faith, voluntary, no gross negligence, within training)

SKILL PRACTICE: PPE - Glove Donning and Doffing [S1] (10 min)

CSA 6.3

This is an assessed skill. All participants must demonstrate competency.

Step 1: Silent Demonstration

Perform the complete glove donning and doffing procedure without speaking. Let participants observe the full sequence.

Step 2: Demonstration with Explanation

Instructor Script (Donning):

"I'm going to show you the proper technique for putting on gloves.

First, I inspect the gloves for any tears or holes. I check both gloves. Any damage means I get a new pair.

I select the appropriate size—too big and they'll slip, too small and they'll tear.

I insert my dominant hand first, pulling the glove up over my wrist.

Then I use my gloved hand to pull on the second glove.

Notice I'm careful not to touch the outside of the gloves with my bare skin."

Instructor Script (Doffing):

"Removing gloves correctly is actually MORE important than putting them on, because this is when contamination can occur.

Remember: Glove-to-glove, skin-to-skin.

I pinch the outside of one glove near the wrist—never touch my skin.

I pull it off, turning it inside-out as I remove it.

I hold this removed glove in my still-gloved hand.

Now I slide my bare finger UNDER the remaining glove at the wrist.

I peel it off inside-out, over the first glove.

Now the contaminated surfaces are all contained inside.

I dispose of the gloves in an appropriate container and wash my hands immediately."

Step 3: Guided Practice

Have all participants practice with you guiding each step verbally.

Step 4: Independent Practice

Allow participants to practice 2-3 times independently. Observe and provide individual feedback.

Critical Steps Checklist (S1)
  1. Inspects gloves for damage before donning
  2. Selects appropriate size
  3. Dons gloves without contaminating outer surface
  4. Removes first glove using glove-to-glove method
  5. Removes second glove using skin-to-skin method
  6. Disposes of gloves appropriately
  7. Washes hands after removal

SKILL PRACTICE: Scene Assessment [S1, S2] (10 min)

CSA 6.4
Pre-Practice Setup

Instructor Script:

"Scene assessment is your foundation skill. Every emergency response starts here. If you skip this step or do it poorly, you could become a victim yourself—or miss critical information.

We're going to practice approaching emergency scenes systematically. I want you to verbalize your thought process out loud as you approach."

Practice Area Setup:

  1. Clear an area approximately 3m x 3m
  2. Place manikin or role-playing volunteer in various positions
  3. Add props to simulate hazards (wet floor sign, extension cords, etc.)
  4. Position "bystanders" (other participants) nearby
Step 1: Silent Demonstration (2 minutes)

Approach the scene silently. Demonstrate:

  1. Stop at safe distance
  2. Scan environment 360 degrees
  3. Look at the person's position
  4. Look for environmental clues
  5. Note bystander presence
  6. Don gloves before approach
Step 2: Demonstration with Explanation (3 minutes)

Instructor Script:

"Watch me approach this scene. I'll talk through my thought process.

STOP AND SCAN: First, I stop before entering the area. I don't rush in—even though my instinct tells me to help immediately. I take 5-10 seconds to look around.

CHECK FOR HAZARDS: I scan 360 degrees. What could hurt me?

  1. Is there traffic? No, we're indoors.
  2. Fire or smoke? No.
  3. Electrical hazards? I see a power strip but no exposed wires, nothing in water.
  4. Chemical spills? No unusual smells, no puddles of unknown substance.
  5. Violence? No aggressive individuals, no weapons visible.
  6. Structural hazards? Ceiling and walls intact.

The scene is safe for me to enter.

OBSERVE THE PERSON: I look at the person. They're lying face down on the floor. I don't see any blood pooling. Their position doesn't suggest they fell from height.

GATHER INFORMATION: I ask the bystander: 'Did you see what happened?' They tell me the person was standing, then suddenly collapsed.

PROTECT MYSELF: I put on my gloves before making any contact.

APPROACH: Now I move to the person to begin my primary assessment."

Step 3: Guided Practice (5 minutes)

Instructor Script:

"Everyone find a partner. One person will be the 'injured worker,' lying on the floor. The other approaches as the first aider. Bystanders, position yourself near the scene.

First aiders, I want you to verbalize every step: 'I stop and scan. I check for traffic—none. Fire—none. Electrical—none. Chemical—none. Violence—none.'

Then observe the person. Then ask your bystander what happened. Then put on gloves and approach.

Let's try this together. Ready? Stop and scan..."

Walk participants through the sequence verbally while they practice.

Transition Script

"Once you've determined the scene is safe, you need to quickly assess the person for life-threatening conditions. We do this using the Primary Survey—LABC."

Key Teaching Points

L - Level of Consciousness [S3]:

Instructor Script:

"Approach the person and check if they respond. Tap both shoulders firmly and shout: 'Are you okay? Can you hear me?'"

AVPU Scale:

Table showing Letter and Response
Letter Response Meaning
A Alert Eyes open, talking normally, aware of surroundings
V Verbal Responds to your voice but confused or slow
P Pain Responds only to touch/pinch (sternal rub)
U Unresponsive No response at all

If Unresponsive: Immediately have someone call 911 and get an AED.

A - Airway [S4]:

Is the airway open?

Instructor Script:

"If the person is unresponsive, you need to open their airway. The most common cause of airway obstruction in an unresponsive person is the tongue falling back."

Head-Tilt Chin-Lift Technique:

  1. Place one hand on forehead
  2. Place two fingers under the BONY part of the chin (not the soft tissue)
  3. Tilt the head back gently
  4. Lift the chin to open the airway

Contraindication: If you suspect spinal injury based on mechanism, do NOT perform head-tilt chin-lift. Use jaw thrust instead (taught at Intermediate level). At Basic level, if spinal injury is suspected and the person is breathing, maintain their position and wait for EMS.

B - Breathing [S5]:

Is the person breathing normally?

Look-Listen-Feel for 5-10 seconds:

  1. LOOK for chest rise
  2. LISTEN for breath sounds
  3. FEEL for air from nose/mouth

Normal vs. Abnormal Breathing:

Table showing Normal and Abnormal
Normal Abnormal
Chest rises and falls regularly No visible chest movement
Quiet breathing sounds Gasping, gurgling, snoring
Comfortable, effortless Labored, strained

CRITICAL - Agonal Breathing:

"Agonal gasps are NOT normal breathing. They look like occasional gasps, fish-out-of-water movements. If you see this, treat the person as NOT BREATHING and begin CPR immediately."

C - Circulation [S6]:

Check for signs of circulation and severe bleeding:

Instructor Script:

"At Basic level, we don't check for a pulse—that's too unreliable without training. Instead, we look for signs of circulation and severe bleeding."

Quick Body Scan:

  1. Look for blood pooling
  2. Look for blood soaking through clothing
  3. Note skin color (pale = shock concern)
  4. Note obvious deformities

If severe bleeding is found: Apply direct pressure IMMEDIATELY.

Life-Threatening Conditions [S7]:

After LABC, you can identify:

  1. Unresponsive (call 911, may need CPR)
  2. Not breathing normally (begin CPR)
  3. Severe bleeding (apply direct pressure)

SKILL PRACTICE: Primary Survey [S3-S7] (20 min)

CSA 6.4

This is an assessed skill sequence. All participants must demonstrate competency.

Pre-Practice Setup

Instructor Script:

"The Primary Survey is your systematic approach to identifying life-threatening conditions. You'll use LABC every single time you respond to an emergency.

  1. L = Level of consciousness
  2. A = Airway
  3. B = Breathing
  4. C = Circulation

This sequence is designed to catch the problems that will kill someone in minutes. Let's practice until it becomes automatic."

Equipment Setup:

  1. Adult manikins positioned on floor
  2. Blankets or mats for participant comfort
  3. Pocket masks available but not required for this practice
  4. Timer visible to instructor
Step 1: Silent Demonstration (2 minutes)

Perform the complete primary survey on a manikin without speaking:

  1. Scene assessment (brief scan)
  2. Approach and kneel beside manikin
  3. Tap shoulders, lean in close
  4. Point to "bystander," gesture for phone
  5. Head-tilt chin-lift
  6. Look at chest for 5-10 seconds (count on fingers)
  7. Quick body scan (head to toe visual sweep)
  8. Position for CPR (but don't start)
Step 2: Demonstration with Explanation (5 minutes)

Instructor Script:

"I've already assessed the scene—it's safe. I'm wearing my gloves. Now watch me perform the Primary Survey.

L - LEVEL OF CONSCIOUSNESS:

I approach and check for response. I tap both shoulders firmly—not gently, firmly—and say loudly: 'Are you okay? Can you hear me?'

Why both shoulders? Because if they have a hearing loss in one ear, they might not hear me on one side.

I'm watching for ANY response: eye opening, movement, sound. Even a groan or twitch means they're responding.

[PAUSE] No response. This person is Unresponsive on the AVPU scale.

ACTIVATE EMS:

I look up, make eye contact with someone, and point directly at them: 'YOU! In the red shirt! Call 911. Tell them we have an unresponsive person who may not be breathing. Get the AED. Come back and tell me when you've done it.'

Why be so specific? In emergencies, people freeze. General calls for help often go unanswered—everyone thinks someone else will do it. By pointing and making eye contact, I've assigned responsibility to a specific person.

A - AIRWAY:

Now I open the airway. My hand goes on the forehead. Two fingers go under the bony part of the chin—not the soft tissue, the bony jaw. I tilt the head back gently while lifting the chin.

Why does this work? When someone is unresponsive, their muscle tone relaxes. The tongue, which is attached to the jaw, falls back and blocks the airway. Head-tilt chin-lift pulls the jaw forward and lifts the tongue off the back of the throat.

B - BREATHING:

I check for breathing for 5-10 seconds. I look at the chest for rise and fall. I don't put my ear to their mouth—that wastes time and is unreliable. I just watch the chest.

I'm counting: one one-thousand, two one-thousand, three one-thousand, four one-thousand, five one-thousand...

I see no chest rise. I hear no breathing sounds. I feel no air movement.

This person is NOT breathing normally.

CRITICAL POINT - AGONAL GASPS:

What if I saw occasional, irregular gasps? Snoring sounds? Jaw movement without real breathing? That's called agonal breathing—and it is NOT normal breathing.

If you see agonal gasps, treat the person as NOT BREATHING and begin CPR immediately. This is the most commonly missed sign of cardiac arrest.

C - CIRCULATION:

Before I start CPR, I do a quick scan for severe bleeding. I look at the head, neck, chest, abdomen, pelvis, legs. I'm looking for blood pooling, blood soaking through clothing, or obvious deformities.

No major bleeding visible. No obvious deformities.

DECISION POINT:

My assessment is complete. This person is:

  1. Unresponsive (U on AVPU)
  2. Not breathing normally
  3. No severe bleeding

My next action is: Begin CPR immediately.

If I had found severe bleeding, I would control that first with direct pressure, then continue with CPR or other care as needed."

Step 3: Guided Practice (5 minutes)

Instructor Script:

"Everyone position yourselves beside a manikin. We'll go through the Primary Survey together, step by step. Say everything out loud.

Ready? Let's begin.

LEVEL OF CONSCIOUSNESS: Tap both shoulders. Say: 'Are you okay? Can you hear me?'

[PAUSE] No response. Say it: 'This person is unresponsive.'

ACTIVATE EMS: Point to your neighbor. Say: 'You, call 911 and get the AED. Come back and tell me when you've done it.'

AIRWAY: Hand on forehead. Fingers under the chin. Head-tilt chin-lift. Open that airway.

Why Do Airways Close?

Instructor Script:

"Now here's the critical question: Why does an unresponsive person's airway close? The answer is simple—muscles relax.

The tongue is a large muscle attached to the lower jaw. When you're awake, muscle tone keeps it in place. But when you're unconscious, that muscle relaxes, and gravity pulls the tongue backward, blocking the airway.

This is why the TONGUE is the most common cause of airway obstruction in an unresponsive person—not food, not vomit, but the person's own tongue."

Key Teaching Points

When to Open an Airway [K1]:

Indications:

  1. Person is unresponsive
  2. Person is not breathing normally
  3. Snoring or gurgling sounds are present

  5. Visible obstruction in mouth

Contraindications and Precautions [K1]:

Instructor Script:

"There are situations where the standard head-tilt chin-lift is NOT safe. You need to know these."

Table showing Contraindication and Reason
Contraindication Reason Alternative
Suspected spinal injury Could worsen spinal damage Jaw thrust (Intermediate skill) or keep still

Mechanisms Suggesting Spinal Injury:

  1. Falls from height (>6.5 meters / 20 feet)
  2. Motor vehicle collisions
  3. Diving accidents
  4. Direct trauma to head, neck, or back
  5. Person complains of neck pain or tingling/numbness

Basic Level Guidance:

"At Basic level, if you suspect spinal injury and the person IS breathing, do NOT move them. Keep them still and wait for EMS. If they are NOT breathing and you cannot perform jaw thrust, the airway takes priority—the risk of death from no airway outweighs the risk of spinal movement."

The Head-Tilt Chin-Lift Technique [S1]:

Instructor Demonstration Script:

"This is the cornerstone airway skill. I'll demonstrate in slow motion first, then at normal speed.

Step 1: I place the palm of one hand on the person's forehead. I'm using firm, flat contact.

Step 2: I place TWO fingers of my other hand under the BONY part of the chin. This is the chin bone, NOT the soft tissue under the jaw. Pressing on soft tissue can actually close the airway.

Step 3: I push down gently on the forehead while lifting the chin UP and FORWARD. This lifts the tongue away from the back of the throat.

Step 4: I hold this position while I check for breathing—look, listen, feel for 5-10 seconds.

Notice the neutral plus position—the head is tilted back just enough to lift the chin. Not too far back, not too little. If the person were looking at something, they'd be looking slightly above horizontal."

Common Errors to Avoid:

Table showing Error and Problem
Error Problem Correction
Fingers on soft tissue Closes the airway Use chin bone only
Too little tilt Airway remains closed Ensure adequate tilt
Too much tilt Hyperextension in elderly/children Age-appropriate positioning
Lifting by the neck Dangerous, ineffective Lift chin only
Releasing position Airway closes again Maintain throughout

Clearing the Airway:

Instructor Script:

"Sometimes when you open the airway, you'll see something in the mouth. If you can SEE it, you can remove it. Use a finger sweep—hook your finger and sweep along the cheek."

CRITICAL - NEVER do blind finger sweeps:

"If you CANNOT see an object, do NOT put your fingers in the mouth. You could push the object deeper, making things worse. Only remove what you can clearly see."

Maintaining the Airway:

  1. Continue to hold head-tilt chin-lift during rescue breathing
  2. If person is breathing but unresponsive, place in recovery position
  3. If providing CPR, briefly release to give compressions but return for breaths

Topic 3: Recovery Position (10 min)

CSA 6.5

Competencies Addressed: S1 (maintaining an airway)

Transition Script

"You've learned to open an airway. But what if someone is unresponsive but IS breathing normally? You can't hold their head forever. The recovery position is how we keep an airway open when we need our hands free."

Key Teaching Points

When to Use the Recovery Position:

  1. Person is unresponsive
  2. Person IS breathing normally
  3. No suspected spinal injury
  4. You need to leave them (to get help) or use your hands

Instructor Script:

"The recovery position does three things: keeps the airway open, allows fluids to drain from the mouth, and prevents the person from rolling onto their back."

Recovery Position Technique:

Instructor Demonstration Script:

"I'll demonstrate step by step.

Step 1: I kneel beside the person. I straighten their legs.

Step 2: I take the arm nearest to me and place it at a right angle to their body, palm facing up. This will support the head.

Step 3: I take the far arm and bring it across their chest, placing the back of their hand against their near cheek. I hold it there.

Step 4: With my other hand, I reach across and grab the far knee, lifting the leg.

Step 5: I pull the knee toward me. The person rolls onto their side, and I use their own leg as a brake to stop them rolling onto their stomach.

Step 6: I adjust the top leg so the knee is at a right angle—this stabilizes the position.

Step 7: I tilt the head back slightly to keep the airway open.

Step 8: I check: is the airway open? Is the mouth positioned to let fluids drain? Are they stable and not rolling?"

Consideration Action
Pregnant person Place on LEFT side (prevents compression of blood vessels)
Multiple injuries Use the least injured side if possible
Prolonged time Switch sides every 30 minutes to prevent nerve damage
Vomiting Ensure mouth is positioned downward for drainage

  1. Suspected spinal injury (keep flat, immobilize)
  2. Not breathing (start CPR)
  3. Need to monitor or provide ongoing care

Common causes in adults:

  1. Eating too fast
  2. Not chewing food adequately
  3. Talking or laughing while eating
  4. Alcohol consumption (impairs swallowing reflex)
  5. Dentures (reduce ability to feel food)
  6. Medical conditions affecting swallowing

  1. Rushed break times
  2. Eating while distracted
  3. Poor-fitting dental work

Topic 5: Responsive Choking Care (12 min)

CSA 6.5

Competencies Addressed: S2

Transition Script

"You've identified severe choking in a responsive adult. They cannot speak, cannot cough effectively, and their face is turning red. What do you do? The answer is the 5-and-5 technique."

Key Teaching Points

The 5-and-5 Technique:

Instructor Script:

"In Canada, we use a combination of back blows and abdominal thrusts. Why both? Because they work in different ways. Back blows create a sudden pressure change in the airway. Abdominal thrusts compress the air in the lungs, forcing it up to expel the object. By alternating, we maximize our chances of success."

Back Blows (5 blows):

Instructor Demonstration Script:

"Watch my positioning. I stand beside and slightly behind the person.

I support their chest with one hand and lean them forward at the waist. This positions gravity to help the object come OUT, not go deeper.

With the heel of my other hand, I give 5 firm blows directly between the shoulder blades. Each blow is distinct—not a push, but a sharp strike.

After each blow, I check: Has the object come out? Can they breathe? If not, I continue."

Abdominal Thrusts (5 thrusts):

Instructor Demonstration Script:

"Now I move behind them. I reach around their waist.

I make a fist with one hand. The thumb side of my fist goes just ABOVE the belly button, but BELOW the sternum—the breastbone. Feel that soft spot between the belly button and the ribs? That's where my fist goes.

I grasp my fist with my other hand.

I give 5 quick thrusts—inward and UPWARD. Think of a 'J' shape motion. I'm compressing the diaphragm to force air up.

After the 5 thrusts, I check again."

The Complete 5-and-5 Sequence:

  1. 5 back blows
  2. Check
  3. 5 abdominal thrusts
  4. Check
  5. Repeat until:
    1. Object is expelled, OR
    2. Person can breathe/cough, OR
    3. Person becomes unresponsive

Modifications for Special Populations:

Table showing Population and Modification
Population Modification Reason
Pregnant person Use CHEST thrusts instead of abdominal Fetus protection
Obese person Use CHEST thrusts if cannot reach around Anatomical necessity
Wheelchair user Perform from behind chair if accessible Adapt to position
Self-choking Self-administered abdominal thrusts or use chair back When alone

Chest Thrusts Technique:

Instructor Script:

"For chest thrusts, stand behind the person. Place your fist in the CENTER of the chest, on the sternum—the same spot you'd give CPR. Give quick, backward thrusts into the chest."

Post-Choking Care:

Instructor Script:

"Even if the object comes out and the person seems fine, they should see a doctor. Why? Abdominal thrusts can cause internal injuries—bruising to organs, cracked ribs. And any aspirated material could cause pneumonia later."

Document:

  1. What happened
  2. What actions you took
  3. Person's condition after
  4. Recommendation for medical follow-up

Recognizing the Transition:

When Choking Victim Becomes Unresponsive:

Immediate Actions:

Instructor Demonstration Script:

Step 1: Lower them safely.
I support their weight. I guide them to the ground. I do NOT let them fall—that could cause additional injuries. I lower them onto their back.

Step 2: Call 911.
If no one has called yet, I shout: 'Call 911! I need an AED!' If I'm alone, I make the call before continuing.

Step 3: Begin CPR immediately.
I do NOT check for a pulse. In choking, we assume there IS a pulse, but without oxygen, it won't matter. I start chest compressions.

Step 4: Modified CPR sequence.
This is the key difference from regular CPR. Every time I go to give breaths, BEFORE attempting the breaths, I LOOK IN THE MOUTH.

I give 30 compressions.
I open the airway—head-tilt chin-lift.
I look in the mouth. Can I SEE the object?

If YES—I do a finger sweep to remove it.
If NO—I do NOT sweep blindly.

I attempt 2 rescue breaths.
If the chest rises—the object is cleared! Continue CPR if needed.
If the chest does NOT rise—I reposition the head and try again.
If still no chest rise—back to compressions.

I continue 30 compressions, mouth check, 2 breaths, until the object clears, the person responds, or EMS arrives."

Topic 1: Respiratory System Overview (10 min)

CSA 6.6

Competency Addressed: A1 (Awareness)

Opening Script

"Before we learn how to help someone with a breathing emergency, let's understand how breathing works. This will help you recognize when something is wrong and understand why certain interventions help."

Key Teaching Points

The Respiratory System [A1]:

Instructor Script:

"The respiratory system has one main job: getting oxygen into your blood and removing carbon dioxide. Let me walk you through the journey air takes when you breathe."

Air Pathway:

  1. Nose/Mouth → Air enters, filtered, warmed, humidified
  2. Pharynx → Throat area, shared with digestive system
  3. Larynx → Voice box, contains vocal cords
  4. Trachea → Windpipe, reinforced with cartilage rings
  5. Bronchi → Two main branches into each lung
  6. Bronchioles → Smaller branches, like tree branches
  7. Alveoli → Tiny air sacs where gas exchange occurs (about 300 million)

Teaching Tip: "Use your hands to demonstrate the branching structure. Start with your arm (trachea) and spread your fingers (bronchi/bronchioles)."

How Breathing Works:

Instructor Script:

"Breathing is controlled by the diaphragm, a dome-shaped muscle below your lungs. When you breathe IN, the diaphragm contracts and flattens, pulling air into the lungs. When you breathe OUT, the diaphragm relaxes and air is pushed out. This happens automatically about 12-20 times per minute in a resting adult."

Normal Breathing Rates by Age:

Table showing Age Group and Normal Breaths/Minute
Age Group Normal Breaths/Minute Characteristics
Adult (12+ years) 12-20 Quiet, regular, effortless
Child (1-12 years) 15-30 Faster than adults
Infant (<1 year) 25-50 Fastest, primarily belly breathing

Critical Teaching Point:

"At Basic level, you don't need to count exact respirations. You need to recognize: Is this person breathing normally, or is something wrong?"

What is Asthma? [A2]:

Instructor Script:

"Asthma is a chronic condition where the airways become inflamed, narrow, and produce extra mucus. During an asthma attack, these airways constrict suddenly, making it very hard to breathe—especially to breathe OUT. Think of it like breathing through a narrow straw."

Asthma Triggers (Common):

  1. Allergens (dust, pollen, pet dander, mold)
  2. Respiratory infections (cold, flu)
  3. Exercise (especially in cold, dry air)
  4. Smoke, air pollution, strong odors
  5. Stress and strong emotions
  6. Cold air
  7. Certain medications (aspirin, NSAIDs)

Signs and Symptoms of an Asthma Attack:

Instructor Script:

"A person having an asthma attack typically shows these signs:"

Table showing Sign/Symptom and What You'll Notice
Sign/Symptom What You'll Notice
Wheezing Whistling sound, especially breathing out
Coughing Persistent, may be worse at night
Shortness of breath Difficulty breathing, can't catch breath
Chest tightness Feels like band around chest
Difficulty speaking Short, choppy sentences

Teaching Tip: "Ask: 'Who here has asthma or knows someone with asthma?' Use their experiences to make the lesson more relevant."

Helping Someone with Asthma:

Instructor Script:

"Most people with asthma carry a rescue inhaler—usually blue. They know how to use it. Your job is to help them stay calm, help them find and use their inhaler, and call 911 if they don't improve."

Steps to Assist:

  1. Stay calm and help the person stay calm
  2. Help them sit in a comfortable position (usually upright)
  3. Ask if they have an inhaler
  4. Help them use their inhaler if needed
  5. Loosen any tight clothing
  6. Monitor their condition

When to Call 911:

  1. No inhaler available
  2. Inhaler not helping after 5-10 minutes
  3. Severe distress (can't speak, blue color)
  4. Person has never had asthma before (could be something else)
  5. Person is getting worse despite treatment

Instructor Script:

"A rescue inhaler, often called a 'puffer,' contains medication that quickly relaxes the muscles around the airways, opening them up. The person usually takes 1-2 puffs, waits a minute, and can repeat if needed. At Basic level, you don't administer medication—you assist the person in using their OWN prescribed medication."

Understanding Cardiovascular Conditions [A8, A9]:

CSA 6.7 CSA 6.7

Instructor Script:

"Before we discuss heart attacks and cardiac arrest, let's understand two common cardiovascular conditions you should be aware of:"

Arteriosclerosis (Hardening of the Arteries) [A8]:

  1. Definition: A condition where arteries become thick, stiff, and narrowed due to plaque buildup
  2. Why it matters: Arteriosclerosis is the underlying cause of most heart attacks and strokes
  3. Risk factors: High blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, diabetes, obesity, lack of exercise

  1. Definition: Chest pain or discomfort caused by reduced blood flow to the heart muscle
  2. Types:

  3. Signs: Chest pressure, squeezing, or tightness; may radiate to arm, neck, or jaw
  4. First aider response: Help person rest, assist with their prescribed nitroglycerin if available, call 911 if pain does not resolve or if this is new/different from usual angina

Key Distinction: Stable angina typically resolves within 5 minutes with rest or medication. If chest pain persists beyond 5 minutes or is accompanied by sweating, nausea, or shortness of breath, treat as a potential heart attack and call 911 immediately.

Opening Script

Instructor Script:

"Now that we understand the circulatory and nervous systems, we need to discuss shock—a life-threatening condition that occurs when these systems fail.

Key Teaching Points

Definition of Shock [A3]:

  1. Shock is inadequate tissue perfusion (blood flow to organs)
  2. Body organs are not receiving enough blood and oxygen
  3. Without treatment, shock leads to organ failure and death

Types of Shock (Awareness Level):

Type Cause Common Workplace Situations

Signs and Symptoms of Shock [A4]:

Early Signs:

  1. Anxiety, restlessness, sense of doom
  2. Rapid, weak pulse
  3. Rapid, shallow breathing
  4. Pale, cool skin (check mucous membranes in darker skin tones)
  5. Thirst
  6. Nausea

Progressive Signs:

  1. Sweating (diaphoresis)
  2. Skin becomes mottled or clammy
  3. Weakness, dizziness
  4. Confusion, disorientation

  1. Decreasing responsiveness
  2. Blue or gray skin color (cyanosis)
  3. Slow, irregular breathing
  4. Weak or absent pulse

"The key insight is that early shock looks like nervousness or anxiety. If someone has been injured and seems unusually anxious, pale, and sweaty—think shock. Don't wait for the late signs."

Awareness: Management of Shock

Per CSA Z1210:24, management of shock at the Basic level is an Awareness [A] competency. First aiders should focus on recognition and supportive care:

  1. Address the Cause: Control external bleeding or assist with an auto-injector if needed.

No response. This person needs help.

Why do I point specifically? Because in emergencies, people freeze. Direct commands with eye contact cut through panic.

CHECK BREATHING: I look at the chest for 5-10 seconds. Is it rising and falling normally? I'm NOT putting my ear to their mouth—I'm watching the chest.

What am I looking for? Normal breathing means regular, visible chest movement. No breathing is obvious. But here's what's tricky: agonal gasps.

Agonal gasps are occasional, gasping, labored breaths. They sound like snoring, gurgling, or groaning. This is NOT normal breathing. If you see agonal gasps, you START CPR.

This person isn't breathing normally. I begin CPR.

Why off the ribs? Pressing on ribs increases fracture risk. We want all our force going straight down into the sternum.

BODY POSITION: Arms straight—like pushing a car. Shoulders directly over my hands. This lets me use my body weight, not my arm muscles. You'll fatigue quickly if you bend your arms.

COMPRESSIONS: I push STRAIGHT DOWN, at least 2 inches deep. 100-120 per minute. Listen to the metronome—that's the rhythm. HARD and FAST.

BREATHING: I seal my pocket mask over nose and mouth. Create a good seal. I give ONE breath, lasting 1 second, and watch for chest rise.

Did the chest rise? Yes—that breath went in. I give a SECOND breath.

If the chest doesn't rise, I reposition the airway and try again. But I don't spend more than 10 seconds on breaths.

BACK TO COMPRESSIONS: I go right back to compressions. Time off the chest = time the brain is dying. 30 compressions, 2 breaths. 30 compressions, 2 breaths.

That's one-rescuer adult CPR."

Key Teaching Points

What is Defibrillation? [K2]:

  1. Electrical shock that stops abnormal heart rhythm
  2. Allows heart's natural pacemaker to restart
  3. Only effective for certain rhythms (VF, pVT)
  4. AED analyzes rhythm and advises if shock is needed

Time-Survival Relationship:

  1. For every minute without defibrillation, survival decreases 7-10%
  2. Early AED use (within 3-5 minutes) can result in survival rates of 50-70%

When to Use an AED [K3]:

  1. Person is unresponsive
  2. Person is not breathing normally
  3. AED is available

Instructor Script:

"The AED is designed for anyone to use. When you turn it on, it talks you through every step. Your job is to follow the instructions while continuing CPR when directed."

  1. Power ON - Press the power button

    2. One pad on left side, below armpit
  3. Clear for analysis - Verbally and visually ensure no one is touching the person
  4. Shock if advised - Press the shock button

Module 5 Common Misconceptions

Before summarizing, address these common misconceptions participants may have:

Table showing Misconception and Correction
Misconception Correction
"I need to check for a pulse before starting CPR" At Basic level, check responsiveness and breathing only. Pulse checks waste valuable time and are often inaccurate.
"I might hurt them with CPR or the AED" You cannot make a cardiac arrest worse. Without CPR, they will die. Some rib injuries are expected during effective CPR.
"The AED might shock me" Modern AEDs analyze the rhythm and won't shock unless appropriate. Even if you're touching the person during a shock, it's a mild jolt—not dangerous.
"I should do mouth-to-mouth if I don't have a barrier" Compression-only CPR is acceptable if you're uncomfortable with rescue breaths. Compressions alone still significantly improve survival.
"I should wait to see if they start breathing again" If they're not breathing normally (including agonal gasps), start CPR immediately. Waiting reduces their chance of survival.
"Shock means the person was electrocuted" Circulatory shock and electrical shock are different. We're discussing circulatory shock—inadequate blood flow to organs.

When to Stop CPR

CSA 6.7

Instructor Script:

"An important question participants often ask: When do I stop CPR? Let's go through the criteria."

Continue CPR until one of the following occurs:

  1. The person shows signs of life - They start moving, coughing, or breathing normally. Stop CPR and place them in recovery position. Continue monitoring and be prepared to restart if needed.
  2. EMS takes over - When paramedics arrive and tell you to stop. They will take over advanced care.
  3. An AED tells you to stop for analysis or shock - Stop only during these specific AED prompts. Resume immediately when told to.
  4. You are physically unable to continue - CPR is exhausting. If you become so fatigued you cannot maintain quality compressions, and no one else can take over, you may have to stop. Get help before reaching this point if possible.
  5. The scene becomes unsafe - If hazards develop (fire, structural collapse, violence), you may need to move or stop.

Do NOT stop CPR:

  • Because you think it's been too long
  • Because you see agonal gasps (these are not normal breathing)
  • Because you're unsure if you're doing it right (some CPR is better than no CPR)

Opening Script

Instructor Script:

Key Teaching Points

Understanding the Clotting Process:

Instructor Script:

"When you cut yourself, your body initiates clotting immediately. Platelets rush to the site, blood vessels constrict, and a fibrin mesh forms to seal the wound. This process takes 3-7 minutes for most wounds.

Your job as a first aider is to assist this natural process by:

  1. Reducing blood flow with pressure
  2. Keeping the wound still so the clot can form
  3. Not disturbing the clot once it starts forming

This is why we hold pressure for at least 5 minutes without peeking—lifting to check disrupts the clotting process."

The Three Ps of Hemorrhage Control:

Table showing Principle and Action
Principle Action Why It Works
Pressure Direct pressure on the wound Compresses blood vessels, slows bleeding, allows clotting
Position-at-rest Keep the person lying still Reduces heart rate and blood pressure, minimizes blood flow
Part prevent movement Immobilize the injured area Prevents clot disruption, reduces pain

Direct Pressure Technique [S1]:

Step-by-Step Demonstration:

Instructor Script:

"Watch carefully as I demonstrate proper direct pressure technique.

STEP 1 - PPE: First, I put on my gloves. Body Substance Isolation comes before everything else. I cannot help anyone if I become a casualty from bloodborne pathogen exposure.

STEP 2 - ASSESS: I look at the wound quickly. Where is the bleeding coming from? How severe is it? This tells me if I need to call 911 or can manage this myself.

STEP 3 - DRESSING: I take a clean dressing—any clean, absorbent material works. In a first aid kit, this is sterile gauze. In an emergency, it might be a clean t-shirt.

I place the dressing directly over the wound. Not next to it, not around it—directly on the bleeding source.

STEP 4 - PRESSURE: Now I apply firm, direct pressure using the palm of my hand. Not my fingertips—that's not enough surface area. My palm gives me maximum pressure.

How hard? Hard enough that it would be uncomfortable if you weren't bleeding. You need to compress the blood vessels under the wound.

STEP 5 - HOLD: I maintain continuous pressure for at least 5 minutes. This is longer than you think. I'm going to resist the urge to lift and check.

Why not check? Every time I lift, I disrupt the clot that's forming. It's like peeling a scab over and over—the wound never heals.

STEP 6 - SOAK-THROUGH: If blood soaks through the dressing, what do I do? I do NOT remove it. I add more dressings on top and continue pressing.

Why not remove? The first dressing is now part of the clot. Removing it tears the clot apart and bleeding restarts.

STEP 7 - BANDAGE: Once bleeding is controlled—usually after 5-10 minutes of continuous pressure—I secure the dressing with a roller bandage. I wrap firmly but not so tight that I cut off circulation below.

STEP 8 - CHECK: After bandaging, I check circulation distal to (below) the bandage. I check color, temperature, and sensation in the fingers or toes. If they're turning blue, going cold, or the person reports numbness, the bandage is too tight."

Positioning the Patient:

Instructor Script:

"While controlling bleeding, position the person for optimal outcomes:

  1. Have them lie down if possible—this reduces blood pressure
  2. Elevate the injured limb above the heart if practical (NOT for fractures or embedded objects)
  3. Keep them calm—anxiety increases heart rate and blood pressure
  4. Cover them with a blanket to prevent shock"

Special Situations:

Table showing Situation and Management
Situation Management
Embedded object Do NOT remove. Stabilize in place, apply pressure around the object
Amputated part Control bleeding at stump, preserve amputated part separately
Scalp wound May bleed heavily but direct pressure still works. Beware of skull fracture
Nosebleed Lean forward, pinch soft part of nose for 10-15 minutes

When Direct Pressure Alone Isn't Enough:

Instructor Script:

"At Basic level, direct pressure is your primary tool. But what if it's not working?

Signs that direct pressure isn't controlling bleeding:

  1. Blood rapidly soaking through multiple dressings
  2. Person showing signs of shock (pale, sweaty, confused)
  3. Spurting arterial bleeding that won't slow

Your action: Continue direct pressure AND call 911 immediately. EMS has additional tools including tourniquets and hemostatic agents.

Tourniquets are taught at Intermediate and Advanced levels. At Basic level, you use direct pressure and get advanced help."

Bandaging Principles

Instructor Script:

"Once bleeding is controlled, you need to secure the dressing. Good bandaging holds the dressing in place while maintaining circulation."

Bandaging Technique:

  1. Start distal (away from the heart)
  2. Wrap in overlapping spirals toward the heart
  3. Cover the dressing completely
  4. Secure the end with tape or tucking
  5. Check circulation after application

Too Tight vs. Too Loose:

Table showing Too Tight and Just Right
Too Tight Just Right Too Loose
Cuts off circulation Firm but comfortable Dressing slips
Blue/cold distal extremity Normal color and warmth Bleeding resumes
Numbness/tingling Normal sensation Doesn't hold pressure
Pain increases Pain stable or decreasing Movement of bandage

Important: If circulation problems develop after bandaging, loosen the bandage immediately. A bandage that cuts off circulation can cause tissue death.

Topic 2: Anaphylaxis and Epinephrine (18 min)

CSA 6.10

Competencies Addressed: A2, A3, K1

Opening Script

Instructor Script:

"Anaphylaxis is one of the most dramatic and time-critical emergencies you may encounter. Let's learn how to recognize it and assist someone having a severe allergic reaction."

Key Teaching Points

What is Anaphylaxis? [A2]:

Instructor Script:

"Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially fatal allergic reaction. It can occur within seconds to minutes of exposure to something the person is allergic to. The body's immune system overreacts dramatically, causing a cascade of symptoms that can rapidly become life-threatening.

Unlike mild allergic reactions like sneezing or itchy eyes, anaphylaxis affects multiple body systems and can kill within minutes if not treated."

Common Triggers:

Table showing Category and Examples
Category Examples
Foods Peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, milk, eggs, wheat, soy
Insect Stings Bees, wasps, hornets, fire ants
Medications Penicillin, aspirin, NSAIDs, anesthetics
Other Latex, exercise (rare), unknown triggers

Teaching Tip: "Ask participants if anyone in their workplace has known severe allergies. Discuss how knowing this information ahead of time can save lives."

Signs and Symptoms of Anaphylaxis [A3]:

Instructor Script:

"Anaphylaxis affects multiple body systems simultaneously. Let me walk you through what you might see."

Table showing Body System and Signs and Symptoms
Body System Signs and Symptoms
Skin Hives, generalized flushing, swelling (especially face, lips, throat), itching
Respiratory Difficulty breathing, wheezing, stridor, throat tightness, "lump in throat" feeling
Cardiovascular Rapid or weak pulse, dizziness, fainting, low blood pressure, pale skin
Gastrointestinal Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, diarrhea
Neurological Anxiety, sense of doom, confusion, loss of consciousness

The Biphasic Reaction Warning:

Instructor Script:

"Important: Even if someone seems to recover, symptoms can return hours later. This is called a biphasic reaction, and it's why EVERYONE who has anaphylaxis needs to go to the hospital, even if epinephrine seems to have worked."

Anaphylaxis Management [K1] - THE CRITICAL KNOWLEDGE:

Instructor Script:

"This is the most important thing you'll learn about anaphylaxis: EPINEPHRINE IS THE ONLY EFFECTIVE TREATMENT. Antihistamines like Benadryl are NOT sufficient—they work too slowly and don't address the most dangerous symptoms.

People at risk of anaphylaxis carry epinephrine auto-injectors. Your job as a first aider is to HELP them use their device as quickly as possible."

The Anaphylaxis Response Sequence:

  1. Recognize the emergency - Multiple body systems affected, history of allergies
  2. Call 911 immediately - Even if you have epinephrine
  3. Help with epinephrine - Assist the person to use their auto-injector
  4. Position appropriately:
    1. Sitting up if breathing difficulty is primary symptom
    2. Lying down in comfortable position if shock signs (pale, dizzy)
    3. Do NOT sit them up if they want to lie down—could cause collapse
  5. Monitor continuously - Be prepared for CPR
  6. Second dose - If no improvement after 5-10 minutes and EMS delayed

CRITICAL - There are NO contraindications to epinephrine in anaphylaxis:

"Some people worry about giving epinephrine to someone with heart problems. Let me be clear: if someone is having anaphylaxis, epinephrine is ALWAYS the right choice. Without it, they will die. The risks of NOT giving it far outweigh any risks of giving it."

DEMONSTRATION: Epinephrine Auto-Injector Assistance (7 min)

Competency Addressed: K1

Instructor Demonstration Script:

"There are several brands of auto-injectors. I'll show you the general technique, then demonstrate with specific devices if we have training units.

The Universal Steps:

Step 1: Get the device.
The person should know where their device is. If they're having trouble, help them find it. Look for a tubular case or medical bag.

Step 2: Remove the safety.
Every auto-injector has some form of safety cap or cover. This must be removed before use. Follow the device's specific instructions.

Step 3: Identify the injection site.
The outer mid-thigh is the site. You can inject through light clothing—there's no time to remove pants in an emergency.

Step 4: Inject.
Press the device firmly against the outer thigh until you hear or feel it activate. Hold in place for 5-10 seconds.

Step 5: Remove and massage.
Remove the device and rub the injection site for 30 seconds to help absorption.

Step 6: Note the time.
You'll need to tell EMS when the injection was given.

Step 7: Monitor and prepare for second dose.
If symptoms don't improve in 5-10 minutes and EMS is delayed, a second dose may be needed if available."

Topic 3: Diabetic Emergencies (12 min)

CSA 6.10

Competencies Addressed: A4, A5, A6

Transition Script

Instructor Script:

"Our next topic is diabetic emergencies. With millions of Canadians living with diabetes, there's a good chance you'll encounter someone having a diabetic emergency at some point."

Key Teaching Points

What is Diabetes? [A4]:

Instructor Script:

"Diabetes is a condition where the body can't properly regulate blood sugar. There are two main types:

Type 1: The body doesn't produce insulin. These individuals always require insulin.

Type 2: The body doesn't use insulin effectively. May be managed with diet, oral medications, and/or insulin.

For first aid purposes, you don't need to know which type someone has. You need to recognize when their blood sugar is dangerously low or high."

Low Blood Sugar (Hypoglycemia) vs. High Blood Sugar (Hyperglycemia) [A5]:

Instructor Script:

"These two conditions look different, but honestly, as a first aider, your approach is the same if you're unsure which is happening."

Table showing Feature and Low Blood Sugar (Hypoglycemia)
Feature Low Blood Sugar (Hypoglycemia) High Blood Sugar (Hyperglycemia)
Onset RAPID (minutes) GRADUAL (hours to days)
Skin Pale, sweaty, clammy Warm, dry, flushed
Behavior Shaky, confused, irritable, "drunk-like" Thirsty, frequent urination, tired
Breath Normal Fruity/sweet odor
Danger Immediate - can lose consciousness Serious but less immediate

The Golden Rule of Diabetic Emergencies [A6]:

Instructor Script:

"Here's the rule that will save lives: WHEN IN DOUBT, GIVE SUGAR.

If it's low blood sugar, sugar will help immediately.
If it's high blood sugar, a little more sugar won't cause significant additional harm.

The danger of low blood sugar is immediate—the person can lose consciousness, have a seizure, or suffer brain damage within minutes. High blood sugar, while serious, develops over hours or days.

When you can't be sure which it is, treat for low blood sugar."

Management of Diabetic Emergencies [A6]:

For a RESPONSIVE person:

Instructor Script:

"If the person is awake and able to swallow safely, give them fast-acting sugar."

15-15 Rule:

  1. Give 15-20 grams of fast-acting sugar:
    1. 4 glucose tablets
    2. 1 tablespoon (15 mL) honey or sugar
    3. 1/2 cup (125 mL) regular juice or soda (NOT diet)
    4. 6-8 hard candies
  2. Wait 15 minutes
  3. If no improvement, give another 15 grams
  4. If still no improvement or person becomes unresponsive, call 911

For an UNRESPONSIVE person:

  1. Call 911 immediately
  2. Do NOT give anything by mouth (choking risk)
  3. Place in recovery position if breathing
  4. Be prepared for CPR

Never force food or drink into an unresponsive person's mouth.

CSA 6.10

Transition Script

Instructor Script:

"Seizures can be frightening to witness, but with the right knowledge, you can help effectively. Most importantly, you'll learn what NOT to do—because well-meaning but incorrect actions can cause harm."

Key Teaching Points

What is a Seizure? [A7]:

Instructor Script:

"A seizure is a sudden, uncontrolled electrical disturbance in the brain. Think of it like an electrical storm. During this time, the person has no control over their body movements or consciousness.

Seizures have many causes: epilepsy, head injury, fever (in children), low blood sugar, stroke, drug withdrawal, or unknown triggers."

Types of Seizures [A8]:

Table showing Type and Description
Type Description What You'll See
Generalized Tonic-Clonic (Grand Mal) Most dramatic, whole body involved Rigid muscles, then jerking, unconscious, may bite tongue, may lose bladder control
Absence Brief "spacing out" Person stares blankly, unresponsive for seconds

The Phases of a Generalized Seizure:

Instructor Script:

"A classic seizure has three phases:

1. Aura (not always present): Some people feel a warning—strange taste, smell, or sensation.

2. Active seizure (Ictal phase): The body goes rigid (tonic), then starts jerking (clonic). The person is unconscious and cannot hear you. This typically lasts 1-3 minutes.

3. Recovery (Postictal phase): Gradually regaining consciousness. Person may be confused, tired, have a headache, or not remember what happened. This can last minutes to hours."

Seizure Management [A9]:

DURING the Seizure:

Instructor Script:

"Your job during a seizure is to PROTECT, not RESTRAIN. Watch carefully."

What TO DO:

  1. Stay calm
  2. Time the seizure (start your watch or phone timer immediately)
  3. Clear the area of hard or sharp objects
  4. Cushion the head (jacket, sweater, your hands)
  5. Loosen tight clothing around the neck if possible
  6. Protect from injury without restraining

What NOT TO DO:

Instructor Script:

"These are the common mistakes people make. Avoid them."

Table showing DON'T and WHY
DON'T WHY
Restrain the person You cannot stop a seizure. Restraint causes injuries (dislocations, fractures).
Put anything in their mouth People do NOT swallow their tongues. Objects cause broken teeth, choking, or injury to you.
Try to hold the tongue Your fingers WILL get bitten. Severely.
Give food or drink during seizure Choking risk.
Move the person Unless they're in danger (traffic, water, fire).

AFTER the Seizure:

Instructor Script:

"Once the jerking stops, your care continues."

Post-Seizure Care:

  1. Turn onto side (recovery position) to keep airway clear
  2. Check for breathing
  3. Clear any secretions from mouth (if visible and safe)
  4. Protect their dignity (cover them if they've lost bladder control)
  5. Stay with them
  6. Speak calmly and reassure—they may be very confused
  7. Do not give food or drink until fully alert

When to Call 911:

Instructor Script:

"Not every seizure requires 911, but these situations do:"

  1. Seizure lasts more than 5 minutes
  2. Person doesn't regain consciousness
  3. Second seizure occurs
  4. Person is injured
  5. It's their first seizure
  6. Person is pregnant
  7. Person is diabetic
  8. Person has difficulty breathing after seizure
  9. Seizure happens in water
  10. You're unsure of the cause

Topic 5: Stroke Recognition (FAST) (10 min)

CSA 6.10

Competencies Addressed: A10, A11, A12

Transition Script

Instructor Script:

"Stroke is one of the most time-critical medical emergencies. In stroke, every minute that treatment is delayed, about 1.9 million brain cells die. Learning to recognize stroke quickly can literally save someone's brain—and their life."

Key Teaching Points

What is a Stroke? [A10]:

Instructor Script:

"A stroke occurs when blood flow to part of the brain is interrupted. There are two types:

Ischemic stroke (85%): A clot blocks blood flow. Like a clogged pipe.

Hemorrhagic stroke (15%): A blood vessel bursts and bleeds into the brain. Like a burst pipe.

Both cause brain cells to die. The symptoms depend on which part of the brain is affected."

The FAST Assessment [A11]:

Instructor Script:

"FAST is the easiest way to remember stroke recognition. Let me demonstrate each step."

F - FACE:

"Ask the person to smile. Look at their face. Does one side droop? Is one side of the mouth not moving?"

A - ARMS:

"Ask the person to raise both arms and hold them up. Watch carefully. Does one arm drift downward? Can they not raise one arm at all?"

S - SPEECH:

"Ask the person to repeat a simple phrase, like 'The sky is blue.' Is their speech slurred? Do they use wrong words? Can they not speak at all?"

T - TIME:

"If you see ANY of these signs, note the time symptoms started and CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY. Time is brain. Tell the dispatcher: 'I think someone is having a stroke. Symptoms started at [time].'"

Teaching Tip: Practice the FAST assessment with a partner. Have one person pretend to have stroke symptoms while the other conducts the assessment.

Other Stroke Symptoms:

  1. Sudden severe headache ("worst headache of my life")
  2. Sudden confusion or trouble understanding
  3. Sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes
  4. Sudden trouble walking, dizziness, loss of balance
  5. Sudden numbness or weakness on one side

Stroke Management [A12]:

Instructor Script:

"Your job with stroke is simple but critical: RECOGNIZE it and GET HELP FAST."

What to Do:

  1. Call 911 immediately
  2. Note the time symptoms started (this determines treatment options)
  3. Keep the person calm and comfortable
  4. Do NOT give them anything to eat or drink (swallowing may be affected)
  5. If they lose consciousness, place in recovery position
  6. Monitor breathing; be prepared for CPR
  7. If possible, have someone meet the ambulance

THE TIME WINDOW:

"For ischemic stroke, there's a clot-busting medication (tPA) that can be given, but it must be administered within 3-4.5 hours of symptom onset. After that window closes, the treatment may not be possible. This is why knowing when symptoms started is crucial."

  3. Cool skin with wet cloths
  4. Give small sips of cool water if alert

Heat Stroke Care:

Instructor Script:

"Heat stroke is a TRUE EMERGENCY. The body's cooling system has failed. Without rapid cooling, the person will die."

  1. Call 911 IMMEDIATELY
  2. Move to coolest available area
  3. Remove clothing
  4. RAPID COOLING: Whatever you have - ice packs to neck, armpits, groin; immerse in cool water if possible; spray with water and fan
  5. Do NOT give fluids (swallowing may be impaired)
  6. Prepare for CPR

Heat Stroke kills. Cool aggressively while waiting for EMS.

Cold Emergencies [A17, A18]:

Instructor Script:

"Cold can also be deadly. Let's look at hypothermia and frostbite."

Hypothermia:

Table showing Stage and Core Temperature
Stage Core Temperature Signs
Mild 35-32°C (95-90°F) Shivering, pale, cold skin, slight confusion
Moderate 32-28°C (90-82°F) Violent shivering stops, increased confusion, drowsiness
Severe Below 28°C (82°F) No shivering, very confused or unconscious, weak pulse

Hypothermia Care:

  1. Call 911 for moderate or severe hypothermia
  2. Move to warm environment (handle GENTLY—rough handling can cause heart problems in severe hypothermia)
  3. Remove wet clothing
  4. Warm the core first (not the extremities first)
  5. Wrap in blankets, add warm packs to neck, armpits, groin
  6. Do NOT give alcohol
  7. Do NOT rub or massage cold skin
  8. If no pulse, start CPR and continue until EMS arrives

In severe hypothermia, the heart is irritable. Handle the person very gently.

Frostbite:

Signs:

  1. White, gray, or yellowish skin
  2. Waxy appearance
  3. Numbness
  4. Hard or frozen feeling

Care:

  1. Get out of the cold
  2. Do NOT rub the affected area
  3. Warm gradually in warm (not hot) water
  4. Protect from refreezing (refreezing causes more damage)
  5. Seek medical care

Common Misconceptions to Address

Table showing Misconception and Correction
Misconception Correction
"Antihistamines can treat anaphylaxis" Antihistamines are NOT enough. Epinephrine is the only effective treatment.
"Don't give epinephrine to someone with heart problems" Epinephrine is ALWAYS indicated for anaphylaxis, regardless of heart history.
"Put something in a seizing person's mouth to prevent them swallowing their tongue" You CANNOT swallow your tongue. Objects cause injuries.
"Restrain a seizing person to prevent injury" Restraint causes more injuries than it prevents. Protect, don't restrain.
"Rub frostbitten skin to warm it up" Rubbing damages frozen tissue. Warm gently in warm water.
"Give alcohol to warm up someone with hypothermia" Alcohol dilates blood vessels and actually speeds heat loss.
"Asking about suicide will give someone the idea" Research shows asking directly does NOT increase suicide risk; it may provide relief.

Instructor Script:

  3. Burns larger than their palm

At Basic level, participants need only awareness of two-rescuer pediatric CPR. Skill competency in two-rescuer CPR is taught at Intermediate level per CSA Z1210:24 Clause 6.13.2.3.

Module 10 Summary

KEY POINTS: INFANT AND CHILD CPR
  1. Infant = birth to 1 year; Child = 1 year to puberty
  2. Child CPR: One or two hands, compress 1/3 depth (about 2 inches)
  3. Infant CPR: Two fingers, compress 1/3 depth (about 1.5 inches)
  4. Infant airway: Neutral position (do NOT over-extend)
  5. Infant breaths: Cover mouth AND nose with gentle puffs
  6. Ratio: 30:2 for one rescuer (both child and infant)
  7. Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute (same as adult)
  8. NEVER shake an infant or perform abdominal thrusts

REMEMBER: Children are not small adults. Respiratory problems usually come first. Rescue breaths are critical.
